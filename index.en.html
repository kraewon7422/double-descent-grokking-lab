The textbook says: “complexity means overfitting.”
So why does GPT work so well?

The overfitting & generalization chapter of an intro-ML textbook teaches that as a model grows more complex, its test error rises. This page reproduces two phenomena that break that claim — double descent (the model-size axis) and grokking (the training-time axis) — with real numerical simulation inside your browser, no external data. Every point on every chart is computed right now, on this device.

Starting point — the textbook's claim

The bias–variance trade-off and the ‘U-shaped curve’

The textbook's story goes like this. A model that is too simple can't even capture the rules in the training data, so its error is large (underfitting, high bias); a model that is too complex memorizes even the noise and does worse on new data (overfitting, high variance). So test error traces a U against complexity, and we're told to pick the ‘right complexity’ at the bottom of that valley.

The paradox — if this picture is right, a GPT-scale giant with far more parameters than data should sit at the far right of the U, its worst region. So why, in reality, do bigger models work better?

Modern machine learning's answer is the two phenomena below. The punchline first — the textbook isn't wrong; it just had a domain of validity.

Phenomenon ① — the model-size axis

Double Descent

Keep growing the parameter count P past the data count N and test error doesn't end in a U. It explodes once near P=N, then falls again as you grow further — and lower than the first U's valley. The U appears twice, hence ‘double descent.’

Why it explodes at P=N

At the moment the model can just barely pass through every training point (the interpolation threshold), there is exactly one solution, so it's forced into an extremely contorted function that threads even the single noisy point. An acrobatic feat with zero degrees of freedom.

Why it descends again beyond that

When P>N, infinitely many solutions pass through all training points. The one the learning algorithm picks is the shortest, smoothest — the minimum-norm interpolant. More parameters means more room to ‘pass through smoothly,’ so generalization actually improves. This is implicit regularization.

P < N :  β̂ = (ΦTΦ)−1ΦTy  (least-squares solution)
P ≥ N :  β̂ = ΦT(ΦΦT)−1y  (minimum-norm interpolant)

The second line is the mathematical heart of double descent. The Tab 02 simulation implements exactly these two equations.

Phenomenon ② — the training-time axis

Grokking

Train a small neural net on a simple operation like a + b mod p, and training accuracy hits 100% almost instantly — it has memorized every answer. But test accuracy lingers at chance level for a long time, then suddenly jumps to 100% thousands to tens of thousands of steps later. The moment memorization turns into understanding is printed right onto the graph.

Why the insight is discontinuous

Two kinds of solution compete inside the network. The memorization circuit forms quickly but has large, complex weights. The generalizing circuit (for modular addition, a Fourier / periodic structure) grows slowly but has small, simple weights. Weight decay taxes the size of every weight at every step, so over time the expensive memorization circuit erodes and the cheap generalizing circuit survives. The visible accuracy jumps like a staircase at the moment the two circuits trade dominance.

What if weight decay were 0?

With no pressure to tear down the memorization circuit, grokking never comes. Tab 03 includes this control experiment as a one-click preset — see for yourself.

Connecting the two

Two cross-sections of the same story

Nakkiran et al. (2019) showed that double descent appears not only along the model-size axis but also along the training-time (epoch) axis. Instead of growing the model, training it longer also produces the same “gets worse, then better again” curve. Grokking can be seen as an extreme form of this epoch-axis phenomenon — both experiments ultimately slice the single story of “excess capacity + regularization pressure discovering a simple solution” along two cross-sections: model size and training time.

The textbook wasn't wrong — it had a domain of validity

Look at the Tab 02 results: inside the classical regime where P<N, the bias–variance trade-off holds exactly. Raise complexity and training error falls while test error eventually worsens — just as the textbook says. The textbook's U isn't a ‘wrong picture’; it was ‘the left half of the whole picture.’ Just as Newtonian mechanics yields to relativity near light speed, classical statistics' intuition was a valid approximation within the boundary of the interpolation threshold.

By analogy with human study — cramming the night before an exam is a 100%-training-accuracy memorization circuit. What the grokking experiment shows is that even in that memorized state, if the ‘pressure to find a simpler explanation’ keeps operating, at some moment a phase transition into genuine understanding occurs. The intuition that insight arrives discontinuously rather than gradually is reproduced numerically in the smallest possible neural net.

References

The five papers this experiment stands on

Belkin, Hsu, Ma & Mandal (2019)
PNAS 116(32)
The paper that coined the term ‘double descent.’ First cleanly showed the peak using random Fourier features (RFF). Tab 02 is a miniature of this experiment.
Mei & Montanari (2022)
Comm. Pure Appl. Math. 75(4)
A precise mathematical analysis of double descent in RFF regression. Proves the peak occurs exactly at P/N=1 — the gold dashed line in Tab 02 is this paper's prediction.
Nakkiran et al. (2019)
ICLR 2020
Showed that in real deep learning (ResNet) double descent appears along the training-time axis as well as model size. The bridge between Tabs 02 and 03.
Power et al. (2022)
arXiv:2201.02177 (OpenAI)
The paper that discovered grokking. Reported that a small transformer memorizes modular arithmetic, then suddenly generalizes long afterward.
Gromov (2023)
arXiv:2301.02679
Reproduced modular-addition grokking with just a 2-layer MLP (squared activation), no transformer, and gave an analytic solution. Tab 03's model structure is a miniature of this setup.
Experiment ① — the model-size axis

We grow the parameter count P and measure test error

Data: generate N samples of y = w*·x + ε (noise ε ~ N(0, σ²)) from x ∈ ℝD (default D=15). Model: linear regression on random Fourier features φj(x) = √(2/P)·cos(vj·x + bj). For P<N we solve the least-squares solution; for P≥N the minimum-norm interpolant. Each P is run several times and we plot the median.
